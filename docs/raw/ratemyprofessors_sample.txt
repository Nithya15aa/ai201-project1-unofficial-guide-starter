Q&A based on accessible source purpose and review-site use:

Q1. What kind of student knowledge can Rate My Professors provide?
A1. It can provide student opinions about professors, class difficulty, workload, grading style, clarity, helpfulness, and whether students felt supported in a course.

Q2. Why is Rate My Professors useful for a campus survival guide?
A2. It helps students make class-planning decisions based on peer experiences rather than only official course descriptions.

Q3. What should students look for in professor reviews?
A3. Students should look for repeated patterns across reviews, such as comments about heavy homework, unclear lectures, strict grading, useful feedback, attendance rules, or whether exams match lectures.

Q4. Why should Rate My Professors be used carefully?
A4. Reviews are anonymous and may reflect extreme positive or negative experiences. A single review should not be treated as proof; repeated themes across many reviews are more useful.

Q5. What kind of official information does Rate My Professors supplement?
A5. It supplements course catalogs, degree plans, professor bios, and university evaluation systems by showing informal student reactions.

Q6. What survival-guide advice can be created from this source?
A6. Students should compare professor reviews with their own learning style. For example, a professor described as lecture-heavy may work well for some students but poorly for students who need interactive teaching.

Q7. What text should be copied manually for the final dataset?
A7. Copy only the review comments from specific professor pages. Skip menus, ads, search bars, buttons, filters, login prompts, and footer links.

Q8. What information should be recorded with each copied review?
A8. Record the professor page URL, school name, course code if visible, date accessed, and the main theme of the review, such as grading, exams, workload, lectures, attendance, or extra credit.
